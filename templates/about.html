Technical Methodology

Behind the Decision Forest

Learn how HydroGuard AI integrates advanced supervised classification models with environmental metrics to manage local water resources.

Project Overview

Water resource distribution is a vital component of municipal infrastructure, requiring dynamic division between drinking networks, agricultural irrigation canals, industrial cooling loops, and general domestic systems. HydroGuard AI acts as an advanced gateway, analyzing crucial biochemical parameters from a sample to predict the safest and most efficient path of usage while computing a regulatory Water Quality Index (WQI).

The Dataset

The core classification models are trained on a comprehensive ecological repository consisting of thousands of geolocated freshwater samples collected across multiple sources (rivers, lakes, borewells, reservoirs) during alternating climate seasons. Variables include chemical concentrations, temperature balances, annual precipitation rates, and dissolved oxygen contents.

The Classifier: Random Forest

A Random Forest is a meta-estimator that fits multiple decision tree classifiers on various sub-samples of the dataset and uses averaging to improve predictive accuracy and control over-fitting.

  • Bootstrap Aggregation (Bagging): Each tree in the forest is trained on random subsets of data with replacement, reducing total model variance without escalating bias limits.
  • Feature Randomization: Random splits select from a subset of attributes (like pH, TDS, DO) preventing dominant parameters from over-correlating individual tree pathways.
  • High Scalability: Handles non-linear decision boundaries and complex feature interactions (such as interactions between DO, Temperature, and Rainfall rates) smoothly.

Core Water Quality Parameters

Here are the key diagnostic properties evaluated by the HydroGuard decision engine:

pH Indicator

A measure of water's acidity or alkalinity on a scale of 0 to 14. Standard aquatic ecosystems thrive in 6.5 - 8.5. Deviations cause pipe corrosion or heavy metal leaching.

Total Dissolved Solids (TDS)

Measures aggregate inorganic salts and organic molecules dissolved in water. Values < 500 mg/L are ideal. High TDS induces mineral deposits and salty tastes.

Turbidity

Dictates physical clarity by assessing how floating microorganisms scatter light rays. Standard limits require < 5 NTU. High values shelter bacterial pathogens from UV treatments.

Dissolved Oxygen (DO)

The concentration of free oxygen molecules available to aquatic organisms. Healthy ecosystems require > 6 mg/L. Low DO signals excessive biological respiration or decaying waste.

BOD & COD

BOD assesses organic degradation rate (ideal < 2 mg/L). COD measures direct chemical oxidation load (ideal < 10 mg/L). High values flag toxic waste.

Nitrate & Fluoride

Nitrates enter from fertilizers (limit 45 mg/L); fluorides from rock weathering (limit 1.5 mg/L). High amounts block blood oxygen or induce skeletal fluorosis.

WQI Scoring Scale

The Water Quality Index is calculated using weighted sub-indices for each parameter. The final value maps human-grade safety metrics into descriptive classes:

90 - 100 Excellent (Suitable for Drinking)
70 - 89 Good (Suitable for Domestic use)
50 - 69 Moderate (Suitable for Irrigation)
30 - 49 Poor (Suitable for Industrial cooling)
0 - 29 Very Poor / Unsuitable for Any Use

Future Scope

The roadmap for HydroGuard AI encompasses integrations representing next-generation municipal smart grids:

  • IoT Stream Arrays: Integrating directly with physical sensor arrays deployed along rivers to receive pH, EC, and Turbidity values in real-time, skipping manual forms.
  • Municipal Dispatch Automation: Hooking predictions into water treatment plant valves to automatically route lower quality run-offs to irrigation networks, saving high-grade reserves.
  • Predictive Leakage Models: Combining sensor pressure data with environmental attributes to foresee structural leaks along concrete aqueducts.
